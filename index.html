Scrum Design Process

What is Scrum?
Students often think scrum and agile are the same thing because scrum is centered around continuous improvement, which is a core principle of agile. However, scrum is a framework for getting work done, whereas agile is a mindset. You can’t really “go agile”, as it takes dedication from the whole team to change the way they think about delivering value to the customers’ needs. But you can use a framework like scrum to help you start thinking that way and to practice building agile principles into your everyday communication and work.
The scrum framework is heuristic; it’s based on continuous learning and adjustment to fluctuating factors. It acknowledges that the team doesn’t know everything at the start of a project and will evolve through experience. Scrum is structured to help teams naturally adapt to changing conditions and user requirements, with re-prioritization built into the process and short release cycles so your team can constantly learn and improve. Lastly, after every sprint a retrospective is held to cover the goods and bads that need to be encouraged and fixed for later iterations.

Hardest Part About Scrum
When it comes to the agile process the most difficult aspect is not what I needed to learn, but what I needed to unlearn. I needed to unlearn previous beliefs and the behaviors that are rooted in those beliefs. For example:

  • Unlearn the belief that work can be specified up front. Instead learn that needs emerge, evolve and change.
  • Unlearn the idea that work can be planned in advance. Learn that work is planned, reviewed, and replanned with blinkers on so we only look at the near future.
  • Unlearn that estimates should be praised and schedules are the source of truth and light. Learn that estimates are just estimated, not commitments, and schedule/plan for slack-time.
  • Unlearn that “that's the way we do it around here, you can’t change the system”. Learn that if things are going to get better, then things have to change around here.
  • Unlearn that you should work on a deliverable until it’s finished, and then report back. Learn that daily standups are essential to the cohesive progress of a project.

Most Rewarding Part of Scrum
As second year students, at the beginning of the semester we were completely and utterly clueless regarding how to go on about creating a useful and doable project within 3 months. However, The central theme of Agile is flexibility. So, when we learned new materials from other concurrent courses, and realized how unrealistic some functionalities of our product were, we were responsive to change, even at the last minute, and adapted to it without much disruption. Also, because project deliverables were not set in stone, we were easily capable of reassessing our plans and adjusting our priorities to align with updated goals. Being adaptable means we could deliver consistently and manage clients’ changing requirements(user-stories) effectively. Because of Agile’s methodology to continuously iterate and improve, meaning processes were improved upon each time an interval is repeated, our team was more and more motivated through the period of the semester as we got closer and closer to achieving a minimal viable product. This consistent focus on improvement and quality control is one of the core principles of Agile, and it helped us to create our final working tuner device.

My Biggest Takeaway
I learned what isn’t a retrospective: An agile retrospective is mainly run for the team to discuss what went well and what didn't during the past sprint. It must be kept only for the involved team so everyone feels comfortable to share their perspective. Project retrospectives are an important component of healthily functioning teams. In order to fully benefit from retrospectives, you should keep in mind some limitations and guidelines:
  • Retrospectives do not replace communication or actions taken to fix team or project issues at the time of occurrence.
  • Retrospectives are not an excuse to spend an hour complaining (although it is important to let some venting happen during the session).
  • Don’t turn the retrospective into a witch hunt. Retrospectives are not an opportunity to lecture your team on what they could have done better. Ideally, you would be proactively coaching your team throughout the project.
  • Retrospectives should not be a huge burden on your time and resources. Throughout the 4 sprints, I found 10 to 15 minutes for reflection was sufficient. This gives everyone ample time to discuss each of the broad themes we’ll cover later in our assignment of user-stories and product-backlogs. This also forces people to be wise with the time they have during the retrospective.
  • If some team members feel depressed or unsatisfied because of some practices, give them space to let these feelings out. Discuss them rationally instead of denying them. Don't let the negative attitude control the meeting either: Try to find what causes those emotions and work on it.

ENEL 300 Takeaways

But, Why Scrum?
The scrum framework itself is simple. The rules, artifacts, events, and roles are easy to understand. Its semi-prescriptive approach actually helps remove the ambiguities in the development process, while giving sufficient space for teams to introduce their individual flavor to it.
The organization of complex tasks into manageable user stories makes it ideal for difficult projects. Also, the clear demarcation of roles and planned events ensure that there is transparency and collective ownership throughout the development cycle. Quick releases keep the team motivated and the users happy as they can see progress in a short amount of time.
However, scrum could take time to fully understand, especially if the development team is acclimatized to a typical waterfall model. The concepts of smaller iterations, daily scrum meetings, sprint reviews, and identifying a scrum master could be a challenging cultural shift for a new team.
But, the long-term benefits far outweigh the initial learning curve. Scrum’s success in developing complex hardware and software products across diverse industries and verticals makes it a compelling framework to adopt in any project.

Detailed User Personas are the Foundations…
A deep understanding of a target audience is fundamental to creating exceptional products. User personas helped our team find the answer to one of our most important questions, “Who are we designing for?” By understanding the expectations, concerns, and motivations of target users, it made it possible to design a product that satisfies the users’ needs and therefore is successful. The first step to identifying the user’s needs is empathizing with them. Empathy is a core value if a team wants to make something that is good for the people who are going to use it. Personas enabled us to create an understanding and empathize with the end-users, guitar owners:

  • Gaining a perspective similar to the user. Creating user personas allowed us to step out of ourselves and recognize that different people have different needs and expectations. By thinking about the needs of a fictional persona, we were better able to infer what a real person might need.
  • Identify with the user that the product is designed for. The more we engaged with the user personas and saw them as real people, the more closely we considered them during the design process and wanted to create the best product for them.

Consumers Want to Save the Earth…
According to research conducted by Accenture, Over the past five years, there has been a 71% rise in online searches for sustainable goods globally, according to The Economist Intelligence Unit. Consumers are engaging with sustainable businesses in ways that they previously ignored. This trend isn't just in first-world countries. Consumer satisfaction in developing and emerging economies is also tied to concerns around climate change, and many want businesses to commit to protecting nature and natural systems. In ENEL 300 we discussed the usage of cards and categories of sustainability to better optimize our designs for sustainability(https://sustainabledesigncards.dk/cards-and-categories/):
  • Maintenance: By reducing the amount visits for maintenance of a product, then we can reduce the energy consumption that is carried out during each maintenance session.
  • Modularity/Design for Disassembly: designing a modular product, such that the product doesn't completely rely on the lifespan of one specific component. Modular products contain separable parts (modules) that can be replaced or upgraded individually by the user. By implementing this, we could support the functional lifespan and overall product longevity through the replacement of faulty components, instead of a completely new purchase. Also, This will further enhance the users’ emotional attachment thanks to modules that adapt to the users’ specific needs.
  • Rental Service/E-Shop: Implementing a website dedicated for the renting of a product can minimize the production of excessive products, and correspondingly, the use of those extra resources. They can also make certain products accessible to a wider public. This could be more realistically implemented through the Re-use of products through rental services such as subscription services and leasing.


Prototyping is the First Step to Success
  • Resolve conflicts. If your team members are in disagreement about the product’s design, prototyping allows them to see which features work best and why. The result optimizes the design, producing the best possible product.
  • Adjust the design. On paper or a computer screen, a design’s problems aren’t always noticeable. By creating a prototype, we can actually hold a version of our proposed product and determine what aspects do its job and which ones need refining. This gives us a chance to gather more accurate requirements and obtain market/professional feedback.
  • Gather Requirements. Prototyping allows us to experiment and spot bugs early on in the process. This quantifies how our product would be used, and how each change would impact the end-result. Due to this we can also easily change direction if we find that we have too many features or the design is not user-friendly. During the prototype model, we’re constantly kept updated throughout the process, and our feedback, as a team, is continually incorporated. This can lead to a better overall result for end users.

ENEL 300 Project

What changed about your product, what iterations did you make and why?
Our initial Idea: The microphone captures noise from an instrument and this noise is converted to an electrical impulse and then compared to ideal frequencies of musical notes and then using the blue, red, yellow, and green LED lights, the device displays visual and verbal feedback of how close the note played on the instrument is to the ideal frequency. The speaker plays a chime when a frequency matching that of a musical note is played and volume can be adjusted with the potentiometer. The default setting with none of the buttons pressed is that the device compares what is being played to the closest ideal note and then lights that corresponding light, but with one of the buttons pressed the device only compares what is being played to the note corresponding to the button pressed as seen from the top view.
I think the biggest cut-down that we had to implement to our tuner device was removing the idea of implementing a musical-note selection, with this deletion we also had to narrow down our feedback notes to only be the open-notes on a guitar. This was because we thought it would be much more elegant if we just used a 7 segment display to show which musical note was currently played instead of leds lighting up differently for different ranges. Additionally, each musical note alphabet ranged in overlapping frequency ranges, so we would have needed to use a lot of storage to exactly store the frequencies of all frequencies that are possible for a single musical note, like A, which the AVR couldn’t handle.Furthermore, we also had to remove the verbal feedback, mainly because the AVR didn't have enough SRAM on board for us to store our desired audio file. Additionally, attaching an SSD card would have taken a very long time to learn how to do, as it would have required us to learn about serial communication protocols from scratch.
Lastly, we also adjusted our algorithm for measuring the input frequency, as we initially began by thinking of implementing a sampling algorithm where we would gather a sufficient amount of conversions of data samples in a specific amount of time, and then we would run through the samples to see how many cycles occurred within that given time period. However, we better adjusted this to make our algorithm analyze in real-time by counting the number of cycles immediately after each conversion sample data was available. This adjustment improved our algorithm's response-time and was more accurate, as we could run through more data samples per second to determine our frequency for our input frequency.

My Biggest Takeaway
I really began to improve my prioritization skills in this project. With all the deliverables from one sprint to another, I had to manage my time and my plans in the most efficient way possible. I did this by allocating and delegating the tasks to the best-fitted group-members for the tasks. For example, when allocating the 3D-CAD design of our casing we choose the person with the most experience in solid-works to accomplish this job so as to not increase our tasks and assign it to a member who still has to brush-up or even learn CAD from scratch. Of course, prioritization is a necessary and fundamental skill to have as an engineer, especially when the workload gets heavier than usual at times. I am sure that I will need this eskill in making sure that deadlines are still met while also submitting my best work. For example, next year, I will be taking at least 5 courses per semester, doing extracurriculars, while also having to handle the pressures of finding, applying and preparing for job applications. With all of these tasks, prioritization will be my best friend and asset.

This is one of the basic rules of a team. Otherwise, a team cannot generate profitable ideas, since some team members will not recognize or respect other proposed ideas.
  • Work Should be Assigned as per Interest and Ability: All of us have different abilities and should be given work as per interest for good results. We should keep on learning but when there is a time boundation, we should work smartly for good results.
